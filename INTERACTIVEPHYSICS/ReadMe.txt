Welcome to the Student Tools CD-ROM

On this CD you will find the Interactive Physics(tm) Textbook Version, developed by MSC Working Knowledge, with over 100 simulations of selected worked examples and end-of-chapter problems for Physics for Scientists and Engineers, fifth edition, by Raymond A. Serway and Bob Beichner. You may also build your own models and simulations. You will be able to print the results of your work, but the Textbook Version will not allow you to save or export data. If you need the Save or Export functions, your instructor may purchase a lab pack or site license directly from MSC Working Group by calling 800.766.6615 or via their web site at http://www.krev.com.


System Requirements

Windows(tm) requirements
• 486/66-based PC or higher. Pentium(r) processors are strongly recommended.
• Windows 95 or Windows NT(tm) 4.0
• 12 megabytes RAM; 11 megabytes free hard disk space
• 256 color VGA video board that is MPC Level II Compliant, and a color VGA 	display
• CD-ROM drive

Macintosh(r) Requirements
• 68020-based processors or above, including PowerPC-based systems
• MacOS System 7.1 or above
• 12 megabytes RAM; 11 megabytes free hard disk space
• 256 color display at 640 x 480 pixels; 800 x 600 (or similar) recommended
• CD-ROM drive


Windows(tm) installation

1. Quit all applications running on your machine.
2. Insert the Student Tools CD-ROM into your CD-ROM drive. We assume this is 
	designated as D:.
3. Double-click on My Computer. Make sure your computer is not hiding MS-DOS 	file extensions. (Check under View, then Options, then View under that. 	The box for hiding MS-DOS file extensions should be off.)
4. Double-click on drive D. (The drive-letter of your CD-ROM drive may differ.) 	You may view the ReadMe file. 
5. To install the Textbook Version of Interactive Physics(tm), double-click on the 
	setup.exe icon and installation will begin. Follow the onscreen 
	instructions. When prompted, enter your name and school, and the serial 
	number: IP50T-00001-063019207. You must accept the License Agreement, 
	which will be displayed for you as part of installation.
6. You will be asked whether you want Typical, Compact or Custom installations. 
	In most cases, you should choose Typical; this installs the Textbook 
	Version of Interactive Physics(tm) plus the simulations for selected worked
	examples and end-of-chapter problems for Physics for Scientists and 
	Engineers, fifth edition, by Raymond A. Serway and Bob Beichner. Compact 
	may be used if you only wish to install Interactive Physics(tm) without the 
	additional simulation files. Custom may be used in rare cases if you need 
	to re-install something mistakenly deleted or corrupted on your hard 
	drive.
7. Interactive Physics(tm) can be launched by clicking the icon available through 
	the Start button on your computer (under Programs). (Also see "Opening a 
	Simulation," below.)


Macintosh installation

1. Quit all applications that are running on your machine.
2. Insert the Student Tools CD-ROM into your CD-ROM drive. If necessary, double-
	click the IP5.0 CD-ROM icon. After a few seconds, a window will appear 
	with the "Launch me to install..." icon, a series of chapter folders, a "For 
	Demo Users" folder, and an icon for the graphing calculator.
3. Double-click the icon entitled "Launch me to install..."
4. Follow the instructions which will now appear on your screen.
5. If you'd like, you can manually copy the simulations over to your hard disk. 
	If you'd rather not use up hard disk space, you can run them simply by 
	reading them directly from the CD-ROM. (See "Opening a Simulation," 
	below.)
6. For initial launch of the program, double-click the IP Textbook icon. When 
	prompted, enter the serial number IP50T-00001-063019207.


Opening a Simulation - WindowsTM

1. Click the Start menu, choose Programs, then Interactive Physics. Make sure 
	the CD-ROM is present in the drive.
2. Interactive PhysicsTM will display an intro screen, and then an empty 
	simulation window will open.
3. Choose File, then Open. The dialog box should display the folders within the 
	Interactive Physics directory, including the For Demo Users and IPSOnPC 
	folders. You can choose simulations from within either of these two. (The 
	others are available only to users of site licensed - i.e., "full" - 
	versions of Interactive PhysicsTM. See "Welcome..." for information about 
	acquiring site licensed versions.)
4. Double-click on the IPSOnPC folder, and then the appropriate chapter-folder.
5. Double-click on the appropriate simulation.
6. You can also use My Computer or Windows Explorer to navigate directly to a 
	simulation file; double-clicking on the file will open Interactive 
	PhysicsTM and read in that file.


Opening a Simulation - MacintoshTM

1. Be sure the CD-ROM is present in your CD-ROM drive.
3. The installation will have placed an "Interactive Physics" icon on your hard 
	drive. (You may have moved that icon elsewhere, of course.) Double-click 
	the icon.
4. It will open and display a series of icons. Double-click the "Interactive 
	Physics 5.0" icon.
5. Interactive PhysicsTM will display an intro screen, and then an empty 
	simulation window will open. (You may wish to resize the window. See 
	Macintosh users' notes," below.)
5. Choose File, then Open. Choose Desktop, then the IP50 CD-ROM.
6. The dialog box will then display the simulation folders on the CD-ROM, 
	including the For Demo Users and IPSOnPC folders. You can choose 
	simulations from within either of these two. (Other simulations may be 
	present in folders either on the hard disk or on the CD-ROM, but these may 
	be available only to users of site licensed - i.e., "full" - versions of 
	Interactive PhysicsTM. See "Welcome..." for information about acquiring site 
	licensed versions.)
7. Double-click on the appropriate chapter-folder, then on the appropriate 
	simulation.


Problems?

Please read through the rest of these instructions. If your question is not 
answered, please call one of the technical support numbers listed below.

WindowsTM Users' Notes

1. Remember that you may find simulations (e.g., in the Physics Experiments 
	folder) which the Textbook Version will not allow you to open. A site 
	license or lab pack is required to view those simulations.
2. Unlike the Macintosh version, the Windows-version installer does copy all of 
	the files to your hard disk; you needn't manually copy any of them. 
	However, the CD-ROM must nonetheless be present to use Interactive 
	Physics.

Macintosh Users' Notes

1. Note that, on the Macintosh, you may have a problem moving from one part of a 
	multi-part simulation to the other. That is, if you're looking at "Part A" 
	of a simulation and can click a button on the simulation to go to "Part B" 
	the system may not be able to find part B. If so, just click the "Find" 
	button on the dialog box, and the filename will be displayed in the list. 
	Double-click it to open it.
3. Remember that 256 colors is the recommended setting. Interactive PhysicsTM 
	will run with a color setting greater than 256, but less memory will be 
	available for the simulations.
3. If you see a "Not enough memory..." error message when attempting to open a 
	simulation:

> Try again to open the file; often the file will open on the 2nd or 3rd try.
> Close any simulation windows you might already have open, even empty ones.
> If the above suggestions don't work, then reduce your color display to 256 
   colors.

4. Having trouble finding a simulation (or a folder full of simulations)? 
	Remember that not all of them are automatically placed on your hard disk. 
	Using the File...Open menu options and the dialog box, direct the system to 
	look on the CD-ROM, called IP5.0. If the CD-ROM is in your drive, IP5.0 
	will show up under Desktop.
5. Remember that you may find simulations (e.g., in the Physics Experiments 	folder) which the Textbook Version will not allow you to open. A site 
	license or lab pack is required to view those simulations.
